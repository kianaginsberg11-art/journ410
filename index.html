Jewish life at Cal Poly

Identity, community and belonging on campus through Chabad, student voices and everyday moments.

By Kiana Ginsberg | May 2026

Explore the story ↓

Finding a home away from home

Rachel Newman didn't plan her first visit to Chabad.

During a trip to Cal Poly before enrolling, she and her parents walked up to the house and knocked on the door unannounced. No appointment and no reason to expect much.

"The rabbi and his wife were so welcoming and supportive," Newman said. "They invited us in and sat and talked with my parents and me for 20 or 30 minutes about life and school. I think if I hadn't had that conversation, I don't know if I would be at Cal Poly."

It's a small moment, the kind that's easy to overlook. But sometimes the places that end up meaning the most to us begin exactly like that, quietly, before we even know we needed them.

For many Jewish students, a sense of belonging on campus doesn't always come that easily. At Cal Poly, Chabad — also known as the Rohr Center for Jewish Life — aims to provide exactly that. The organization describes its mission as creating "an open door environment" for Jewish students of all backgrounds, a space that is non-judgmental, accepting, and centered on personal connection.

Through crowded Shabbat dinners, holiday gatherings and everyday conversations, that mission is brought to life. Students gather over homemade meals, celebrate traditions and build friendships that extend beyond campus life.

In recent years, the need for spaces like Chabad has only grown. Following the October 7, 2023, Hamas attack on Israel and the war that followed, antisemitic incidents have risen across the United States, including on college campuses. According to the Anti-Defamation League, there were 9,354 incidents nationwide in 2024 — the highest number ever recorded — including 1,694 on college campuses.

"I had a professor hit the Nazi salute as a joke in class," Taylor said.

At Cal Poly, those national trends shape students' experiences in more personal ways, sometimes showing up in the places you'd least expect them. And even when antisemitism isn't directly experienced, its presence is still felt.

"There's always going to be antisemitism," Newman said. "You need groups and spaces that are going to stand up for you and make sure that if it does happen, it's properly addressed."

There's something particular about finding a place where you don't have to explain yourself. Where the context of who you are arrives before you do, carried in shared history, shared memory, shared identity. That's what students say Chabad offers, not just a community, but the feeling of being known.

Jewish identity is often defined by three core ideas: עַם (am), a people; אֶרֶץ (eretz), a land; and התורה (hatorah), a religion. It's a combination that can be difficult to navigate in the diaspora, especially on a campus where Jews make up a small minority. The complexity of that identity can make it harder to feel fully connected without a space of your own.

"Chabad plays a really big role in my life," Newman said. "I'm there at least once a week, probably more. I've met some of my best friends in college. The events they put on are very safe and welcoming."

Every Friday, that sense of belonging is rebuilt from scratch, groceries carried in, meals prepared, the table set again. It's a routine that asks nothing of you except that you show up.

"Honestly, Chabad is the best thing that has happened to me here," Taylor said.
